## 1. Role-Level Categories

Understanding the organizational role of your audience determines what information to prioritize, how to structure arguments, and how much time you have to deliver your message.

### Executive (C-level, VP, Board Members)

- **Information needs**: Bottom-line conclusions first, strategic implications, ROI projections, competitive impact, risk exposure. They want to know *what decision to make*, not how the analysis was done.
- **Attention span**: 15–20 minutes. Executives allocate fixed, limited time and expect efficiency.
- **Content approach**: Lead with conclusions and recommendations. Use the Pyramid Principle — state the answer, then provide supporting evidence only if asked. Avoid process walkthroughs. Every slide should answer: "What do you need from me?"
- **Framing**: Strategic, financial, and competitive. Translate technical findings into business outcomes.

### Middle Management (Director, Senior Manager)

- **Information needs**: Implementation feasibility, resource requirements, timeline estimates, team impact, cross-functional dependencies. They need to operationalize decisions.
- **Attention span**: 30–45 minutes. Enough depth to plan action, but still focused on practical outcomes.
- **Content approach**: Balance strategy with execution detail. Show phased plans, resource allocation, and ownership assignments. Provide actionable next steps with clear accountability.
- **Framing**: Operational and organizational. Connect strategic goals to team-level execution.

### Team / Individual Contributor

- **Information needs**: Technical depth, step-by-step processes, tool-specific guidance, immediate applicability to daily work. They need to *do the work*.
- **Attention span**: 45–60 minutes. Tolerant of deep dives when the content is directly applicable.
- **Content approach**: Prioritize how-to detail, worked examples, and reference material. Use diagrams, code samples, or walkthroughs. Anticipate edge cases and common pitfalls.
- **Framing**: Tactical and practical. Focus on *how* over *why*.

### External Client

- **Information needs**: Value proposition, competitive advantage, risk mitigation, proof of delivery, ROI justification. They are evaluating whether to trust and invest.
- **Attention span**: 20–30 minutes. Clients are protective of their time and expect polished, persuasive delivery.
- **Content approach**: Lead with value and outcomes. Use case studies, testimonials, and benchmarks as evidence. Address objections proactively. Close with a clear call to action.
- **Framing**: Client-centric and competitive. Every point should answer: "Why does this matter to *you*?"

### Mixed Audience

- **Information needs**: Varies by subgroup. Must satisfy executives, implementers, and potentially clients simultaneously.
- **Attention span**: 30–45 minutes total, but attention peaks vary by role.
- **Content approach**: Layer information using progressive disclosure. Begin with an executive summary (conclusions), then expand into implementation details. Provide multiple entry points — headers and visual hierarchy let each reader navigate to their depth. Use appendix slides for deep dives.
- **Framing**: Tiered. Start universal, then segment.

## 2. Subject Familiarity Matrix

The audience's existing knowledge of your topic determines vocabulary, depth, pacing, and the balance between explanation and assertion.

### Expert

- **Jargon policy**: Use domain-specific terminology freely and precisely. Shared jargon accelerates communication and signals credibility.
- **Content depth**: Skip foundational concepts entirely. Focus on novel aspects, recent developments, edge cases, and open questions. Assume fluency with standard methods and tools.
- **Explanation style**: Dense, analytical. Rely on data, formal models, and direct assertion. Minimize background context.
- **Visual approach**: Data-heavy charts, technical diagrams, and dense tables. Annotations should highlight non-obvious patterns, not explain basics.

### Familiar

- **Jargon policy**: Use domain terminology with brief contextual cues when terms are ambiguous or domain-adjacent. A short parenthetical or appositive phrase is sufficient.
- **Content depth**: Reference known concepts without re-explaining them. Build from shared knowledge into new or complex areas. Moderate depth — cover more ground than with experts, but less than with general audiences.
- **Explanation style**: Bridge from known to unknown. Use conceptual framing ("similar to X, but with Y difference").
- **Visual approach**: Standard charts and diagrams with clear labels. Visuals should reinforce or extend understanding, not introduce concepts from scratch.

### General

- **Jargon policy**: Minimize jargon. When technical terms are unavoidable, define them inline on first use. Prefer plain-language equivalents.
- **Content depth**: Build from common knowledge and everyday experience. Explain core concepts before layering complexity. Allow more time per concept.
- **Explanation style**: Analogies and real-world examples are essential. Connect abstract ideas to tangible, relatable scenarios.
- **Visual approach**: High reliance on visual explanations — infographics, flowcharts, comparison diagrams, and annotated illustrations. Visuals should do the heavy lifting for comprehension.

### Unfamiliar

- **Jargon policy**: Zero jargon. Use only everyday language. If a technical concept is central, explain it through a concrete analogy before naming it.
- **Content depth**: Start from fundamentals. Limit scope to the most essential concepts. Accept that coverage will be narrower in exchange for clarity.
- **Explanation style**: Step-by-step, concrete, and story-based. Use extended analogies and narrative sequences. Build one concept at a time with frequent comprehension checkpoints.
- **Visual approach**: Maximum visual content. Use simple, iconic illustrations, progressive diagrams that build complexity one layer at a time, and photo-realistic examples where possible.

## 3. Decision-Making Tendency Models

Individuals process information and reach decisions through different cognitive styles. Identifying the dominant tendency in your audience lets you match both content and page type selection.

### Data-Driven

- **Preference**: Numbers, charts, benchmarks, statistical evidence, and trend analyses. They trust what they can measure and verify.
- **Content strategy**: Show the data first. Let quantitative evidence lead the argument. Provide source citations and methodology notes. Use comparison benchmarks to give numbers context.
- **Recommended page types**: Data-Chart (for trend and distribution data), Comparison (for side-by-side evaluation), and Content-TwoCol (for paired data with commentary).
- **Avoid**: Unsupported claims, vague superlatives ("significant improvement"), anecdotal evidence presented as proof.

### Story-Driven

- **Preference**: Narratives, real-world examples, case studies, customer testimonials, and emotional resonance. They connect through human experience.
- **Content strategy**: Create an emotional arc. Lead with a relatable scenario, build tension with a challenge, and resolve with your solution. Use specific, named examples over abstract descriptions.
- **Recommended page types**: Quote (for testimonial or expert voice), Timeline (for narrative progression), and Content-Text with visual storytelling.
- **Avoid**: Dry data dumps without context, abstract frameworks without concrete examples, conclusions without a human element.

### Action-Oriented

- **Preference**: Conclusions, recommendations, next steps, and clear asks. They want to know what to do, not why.
- **Content strategy**: Get to the point immediately. State conclusions in bold, then provide minimal supporting context. End every section with a concrete action item or decision point.
- **Recommended page types**: Content-Text with bold conclusions and bullet-point actions, Problem-Solution-Benefit for quick framing.
- **Avoid**: Lengthy background, extensive methodology descriptions, data without interpretation, open-ended discussion.

### Detail-Oriented

- **Preference**: Methodology, process documentation, comprehensive coverage, and thorough evidence chains. They want to see the work behind the conclusion.
- **Content strategy**: Show your process. Document assumptions, methodology, and data sources. Provide comprehensive coverage — they will notice gaps. Use structured layouts that allow systematic review.
- **Recommended page types**: Content-TwoCol (for paired analysis), Composite-Diagram (for multi-part process flows), and Data-Chart with full annotations.
- **Avoid**: Unsubstantiated assertions, skipped steps in reasoning, oversimplified summaries that hide complexity.

## 4. Audience-to-Design Impact Matrix

Combining role level and decision-making tendency produces concrete guidance for framework selection, content density, visual style, and information hierarchy.

| Audience Factor | Framework Choice | Content Density | Visual Style | Info Hierarchy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Executive + Data-driven | Pyramid Principle | Low (1 key point per slide) | Minimal, professional, muted palette | Conclusions-first, supporting data in backup |
| Executive + Action-oriented | SCQA (Situation, Complication, Question, Answer) | Medium | Bold headlines, clean layout, strong contrast | Problem → Solution, immediate next steps |
| Executive + Story-driven | Problem-Solution-Benefit | Low to Medium | Cinematic imagery, minimal text | Emotional hook → resolution → ask |
| Technical + Detail-oriented | Data-Insight-Action | High (dense data, multi-layer) | Functional, clean, grid-based | Evidence-first, methodical progression |
| Technical + Data-driven | Data-Insight-Action | High | Chart-dominant, annotated | Claim → chart → implication |
| Middle Mgmt + Action-oriented | SCQA | Medium | Structured, checklist-friendly | Situation → impact → action plan |
| Mixed + Story-driven | Timeline | Medium | Engaging, visual, human-centered | Narrative arc with multiple depth layers |
| External + Action-oriented | Problem-Solution-Benefit | Medium | Polished, branded, professional | Value-first, proof-second, CTA-third |
| External + Data-driven | Data-Insight-Action | Low to Medium | Client-branded, chart-focused | Evidence of ROI → competitive proof |
| Mixed + General familiarity | Theme-Illustration-Application | Low (surface) → High (appendix) | Clear visual hierarchy, navigable | Summary → detail layers → appendix |

### How to Use This Matrix

1. **Profile your audience** using Section 1 (role) and Section 3 (decision tendency).
2. **Cross-reference** the combination in the matrix above to determine framework, density, and style.
3. **Adjust for familiarity** using Section 2 — shift content density up for expert audiences and down for unfamiliar ones, regardless of the matrix recommendation.
4. **Validate** by asking: "If I showed this deck to one person in the audience, would they find the right depth in the right place on every slide?"
