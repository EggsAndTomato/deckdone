## 1. SCQA {#1-scqa}

**Situation → Complication → Question → Answer**

The SCQA framework, developed by Barbara Minto at McKinsey, leads the audience from a shared understanding of the current state through a complication that disrupts it, arriving naturally at a question your presentation then answers.

### Structure

| Stage | Purpose | Tone |
|-------|---------|------|
| **Situation** | Establish shared context and baseline facts | Neutral, factual |
| **Complication** | Introduce the disruption, challenge, or opportunity | Tension-building |
| **Question** | Frame the central question that arises from the complication | Focused, sharp |
| **Answer** | Present your recommendation or solution with supporting evidence | Confident, persuasive |

### When to Use

- **Audience**: Executives, senior decision-makers, steering committees
- **Purpose**: Strategy recommendations, problem-solving proposals, organizational change
- **Works best when**: The audience already agrees on the current situation but may not see the urgency of the problem or the logic of your solution

### Example 20-Page Outline

**Situation (Pages 1–4)**

|------|---------|-----------|
| 1 | Title slide with framing question | Cover |
| 2 | Industry context and current business model | Content-Text |
| 3 | Key performance metrics and market position | Data-Chart |

**Complication (Pages 5–8)**

| Page | Content | Page Type |
|------|---------|-----------|
| 5 | Emerging market shifts disrupting the status quo | Content-Text |
| 6 | Competitor movements and competitive pressure | Comparison |
| 7 | Internal capability gaps and operational risks | Data-Chart |
| 8 | Financial impact of inaction (scenario modeling) | Data-Chart |

**Question (Pages 9–10)**

| Page | Content | Page Type |
|------|---------|-----------|
| 9 | The central strategic question | Section Divider |
| 10 | Decision criteria and evaluation framework | Composite-Diagram |

**Answer (Pages 11–18)**

| Page | Content | Page Type |
|------|---------|-----------|
| 11 | Recommended strategy overview | Content-Text |
| 12 | Strategic pillar 1: Market expansion | Content-Text |

**Closing (Pages 19–20)**

| Page | Content | Page Type |
|------|---------|-----------|

| 20 | Next steps and decision request | Closing |

## 2. Pyramid Principle {#2-pyramid-principle}

**Conclusion → Supporting Arguments → Data/Evidence**

The Pyramid Principle fronts the conclusion so decision-makers get the bottom line immediately, then builds downward through supporting layers. Every tier answers the question "why?" or "how?" from the tier above.

### Structure

```
            ┌──────────────┐
            │  Conclusion   │   ← Lead with the answer
            └──────┬───────┘
         ┌─────────┼─────────┐
         ▼         ▼         ▼
      Arg 1      Arg 2     Arg 3   ← Three supporting pillars
      ┌──┐       ┌──┐      ┌──┐
      │D │       │D │      │D │    ← Data backing each argument
      └──┘       └──┘      └──┘
```

### When to Use

- **Audience**: Executives, board members, time-pressed senior stakeholders
- **Purpose**: Executive summaries, board presentations, investment committee briefs, any context where the audience wants the answer upfront
- **Works best when**: The audience is skeptical, time-constrained, or likely to interrupt with "what's the bottom line?"

### Example 20-Page Outline

**Conclusion (Pages 1–3)**

| Page | Content | Page Type |
|------|---------|-----------|
| 1 | Title slide with core recommendation | Cover |
| 2 | The bottom line: recommendation and expected outcome | Content-Text |
| 3 | Executive summary of three supporting pillars | Agenda |

**Argument 1 (Pages 4–7)**

| Page | Content | Page Type |
|------|---------|-----------|
| 4 | Pillar 1 claim and rationale | Content-Text |
| 5 | Market data supporting the claim | Data-Chart |
| 6 | Case study or precedent | Content-TwoCol |
| 7 | Implications for this organization | Content-Text |

**Argument 2 (Pages 8–12)**

| Page | Content | Page Type |
|------|---------|-----------|
| 8 | Pillar 2 claim and rationale | Content-Text |
| 9 | Financial analysis and projections | Data-Chart |
| 10 | Risk-adjusted scenario comparison | Comparison |
| 11 | Operational feasibility assessment | Composite-Diagram |
| 12 | Resource requirements | Data-Chart |

**Argument 3 (Pages 13–17)**

| Page | Content | Page Type |
|------|---------|-----------|
| 13 | Pillar 3 claim and rationale | Content-Text |
| 14 | Implementation timeline and phases | Timeline |
| 15 | Quick wins vs. long-term initiatives | Comparison |
| 16 | Capability requirements and gaps | Composite-Diagram |
| 17 | Benchmarks from comparable organizations | Content-TwoCol |

**Evidence Summary and Close (Pages 18–20)**

| Page | Content | Page Type |
|------|---------|-----------|
| 18 | Consolidated evidence across all pillars | Content-Text |
| 19 | Decision framework for go/no-go | Composite-Diagram |
| 20 | Immediate next steps and owners | Closing |

## 3. Timeline {#3-timeline}

**Past → Present → Future** or **Phase 1 → Phase 2 → Phase 3**

The Timeline framework organizes content chronologically, making it intuitive for audiences to follow progression. It can be used for retrospective reviews, forward-looking roadmaps, or combined past-present-future narratives.

### Structure

| Stage | Purpose | Emphasis |
|-------|---------|----------|
| **Past** | Establish what has been accomplished, learned, or changed | Credibility and context |
| **Present** | Describe the current state, active work, and standing | Transparency and momentum |
| **Future** | Outline planned milestones, goals, and vision | Direction and ambition |

### When to Use

- **Audience**: Any audience; especially effective for stakeholders tracking project progress, cross-functional teams, or external partners
- **Purpose**: Roadmaps, project kickoffs and reviews, year-in-review presentations, quarterly business reviews, strategic planning
- **Works best when**: The narrative depends on sequence or progression, or when demonstrating growth and momentum over time

### Example 20-Page Outline

**Past — What We've Achieved (Pages 1–6)**

|------|---------|-----------|

| 4 | Major accomplishments and wins | Content-Text |
| 5 | Lessons learned and pivots made | Content-Text |
| 6 | Performance metrics: then vs. now | Comparison |

**Present — Where We Are Today (Pages 7–12)**

| Page | Content | Page Type |

**Future — Where We're Going (Pages 13–20)**

## 4. Problem-Solution-Benefit {#4-problem-solution-benefit}

**Define Problem → Present Solution → Show Benefits and ROI**

This framework is persuasion-centric. It builds empathy by making the audience feel the pain, offers relief through the solution, then seals commitment by quantifying the upside.

### Structure

| Stage | Purpose | Emotional Arc |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| **Problem** | Make the audience recognize and feel the pain point | Concern, urgency |
| **Solution** | Introduce your approach as the logical remedy | Relief, optimism |
| **Benefit** | Quantify value and make the case for action | Confidence, commitment |

### When to Use

- **Audience**: External clients, prospects, investors, budget approvers, procurement committees
- **Purpose**: Sales pitches, investment proposals, product launches, budget requests, partnership proposals
- **Works best when**: The audience is unaware of or underestimates the problem, and you need to create urgency before offering the solution

### Example 20-Page Outline

**Problem (Pages 1–6)**

| Page | Content | Page Type |
|------|---------|-----------|
| 1 | Title slide | Cover |
| 2 | The landscape: why this matters now | Content-Text |
| 3 | Pain point 1: Operational inefficiency | Content-Text |
| 4 | Pain point 1: Cost and impact data | Data-Chart |
| 5 | Pain point 2: Customer churn and dissatisfaction | Content-Text |
| 6 | Combined cost of inaction | Data-Chart |

**Solution (Pages 7–14)**

| Page | Content | Page Type |
|------|---------|-----------|
| 7 | Our solution: high-level overview | Content-Text |
| 8 | How it works: core methodology or technology | Composite-Diagram |
| 9 | Feature or capability 1: addressing pain point 1 | Content-Text |
| 10 | Feature or capability 2: addressing pain point 2 | Content-Text |
| 11 | Implementation approach and timeline | Timeline |
| 12 | Integration with existing systems | Content-Text |
| 13 | Support and service model | Content-Text |
| 14 | Case study: similar client success story | Content-TwoCol |

**Benefit (Pages 15–20)**

| Page | Content | Page Type |
|------|---------|-----------|
| 15 | Quantified benefits overview | Content-Text |
| 16 | ROI model and payback period | Data-Chart |
| 17 | Before vs. after comparison | Comparison |
| 18 | Total cost of ownership vs. value delivered | Data-Chart |
| 19 | Testimonial or reference proof point | Quote |
| 20 | Proposed next steps and engagement model | Closing |

## 5. Theme-Illustration-Application {#5-theme-illustration-application}

**Introduce Concept → Show Examples → Apply to Audience's Context**

This educational framework moves from the abstract to the concrete. It first ensures the audience understands a concept, then demonstrates it through real examples, and finally helps them apply it to their own situation.

### Structure

| Stage | Purpose | Learning Mode |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| **Theme** | Define and explain the core concept | Conceptual understanding |
| **Illustration** | Bring the concept to life with examples | Observational learning |
| **Application** | Help the audience use the concept themselves | Practical engagement |

### When to Use

- **Audience**: Teams, new hires, cross-functional learners, anyone encountering a topic for the first time
- **Purpose**: Training sessions, knowledge sharing, onboarding, conference talks, educational workshops, change management (new processes or tools)
- **Works best when**: The content involves a new concept, process, or framework the audience has not previously encountered

### Example 20-Page Outline

**Theme (Pages 1–5)**

| 1 | Title slide with framing question | Cover |

**Illustration (Pages 6–13)**

## 6. Data-Insight-Action {#6-data-insight-action}

**Present Data → Extract Insights → Recommend Actions**

This analytical framework respects the audience's need to see the evidence before the conclusion. It builds credibility through transparent data presentation, then layers interpretation, and closes with clear direction.

### Structure

| Stage | Purpose | Analytical Depth |
|-------|---------|------------------|
| **Data** | Lay out the facts, metrics, and observations | Descriptive |
| **Insight** | Interpret what the data means and why it matters | Diagnostic |
| **Action** | Translate insights into specific, prioritized recommendations | Prescriptive |

### When to Use

- **Audience**: Data-driven decision-makers, analysts, management teams, operational leaders
- **Purpose**: Quarterly business reviews, performance reports, analytical deep-dives, post-mortems, OKR reviews, data-driven strategy sessions
- **Works best when**: The audience expects evidence-based reasoning and may challenge assumptions; when data is central to the decision being made

### Example 20-Page Outline

**Data (Pages 1–6)**

|------|---------|-----------|
| 1 | Title slide with reporting period context | Cover |
| 2 | Executive dashboard: key metrics at a glance | Data-Chart |
| 3 | Revenue and growth trends | Data-Chart |

| 5 | Operational efficiency metrics | Data-Chart |
| 6 | Benchmark comparison: us vs. industry vs. target | Comparison |

**Insight (Pages 7–14)**

## 7. Framework Selection Guide {#7-framework-selection-guide}

Use this matrix to quickly identify the best framework based on your audience and purpose.

| | **Executive** | **Technical** | **Mixed** | **External** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Report** | Pyramid Principle — bottom line first, then evidence | Data-Insight-Action — evidence leads, conclusions follow | SCQA — shared context before diving deep | Timeline — progress and milestones are universally understood |
| **Proposal** | SCQA — clear problem→solution logic builds executive buy-in | SCQA — structured logic appeals to analytical minds | Pyramid Principle — conclusion first avoids losing half the room | Problem-Solution-Benefit — creates urgency then offers value |
| **Training** | Theme-Illustration-Application — executives learn through applied examples | Data-Insight-Action — technical audiences want evidence before concepts | Theme-Illustration-Application — moves from theory to practice for all levels | Theme-Illustration-Application — external partners need concept grounding before application |
| **Pitch** | Pyramid Principle — lead with the opportunity, then back it up | SCQA — show you understand the problem space deeply | Problem-Solution-Benefit — emotional arc works across backgrounds | Problem-Solution-Benefit — purpose-built for persuasion and ROI demonstration |
| **Analysis** | Pyramid Principle — start with implications, data is backup | Data-Insight-Action — the natural framework for analytical work | Data-Insight-Action — transparent evidence builds cross-functional trust | SCQA — structured narrative makes complex analysis accessible |

### Quick Decision Flowchart

```
Start → Is the audience time-pressed and senior?
         ├── Yes → Do they already know the problem?
         │         ├── Yes → Pyramid Principle
         │         └── No  → SCQA
         └── No  → Is persuasion the primary goal?
                   ├── Yes → Problem-Solution-Benefit
                   └── No  → Is the content educational?
                             ├── Yes → Theme-Illustration-Application
                             └── No  → Is sequence or chronology important?
                                       ├── Yes → Timeline
                                       └── No  → Data-Insight-Action
```

### Combining Frameworks

Frameworks can be nested. Common combinations:

- **Pyramid Principle + SCQA**: Use Pyramid for the overall deck structure, SCQA for a specific section that introduces a new problem.
- **Timeline + Data-Insight-Action**: Use Timeline for the macro structure (past/present/future), Data-Insight-Action within the "present" section.
- **Problem-Solution-Benefit + Pyramid Principle**: Start with the benefit (Pyramid), then walk through Problem → Solution as supporting arguments.
- **SCQA + Data-Insight-Action**: Use SCQA as the macro arc, Data-Insight-Action within the "Answer" section for evidence-based recommendations.
